I recently built a discrete-time simulator for food-delivery pricing, with the goal of validating a zone-level joint-optimization policy that I thought was "closer to production." The environment ran, three families of policies ran, and I did get numbers — but only after a fairly thorough faceplant: my most sophisticated policy, a rolling mixed-integer program, ended up *worse* than the "do nothing" baseline by over a thousand yuan once I scaled the scenario up.

The root cause turned out not to be the solver at all. It was the objective I fed into it. This post is a short postmortem of that failure and the fix, mostly as a reminder to myself: **an optimizer does not correct your input bias — it amplifies it.**

> Caveat: this is a locally reproducible, fully synthetic simulation. All training data comes from a synthetic DGP, so none of the numbers should be read as real platform revenue. The figures below are from the post-fix medium-scale experiment.

## 1. What the simulator actually models

I was not interested in "given an order table, pick a price." I wanted to study how a pricing action propagates through rider and customer responses and ends up changing conversion, fulfillment, and profit. So the environment has to evolve rather than sit on a static dataset.

Time is discrete, one minute per step. The city is a 2D grid aggregated into operating zones (`zone_id`), and orders fall into three distance buckets: `0_2`, `2_4`, `4_plus`. Each minute, in order:

```text
new pricing window → update rider online/offline → generate latent
requests → policy outputs delivery fee / user discount / rider bonus →
customer decides whether to order → merchant produces food → customer
may cancel via a per-minute hazard → dispatch to nearest rider → rider
accepts/rejects and delivers → settle revenue, subsidy, budget → log
```

The behavior models are written to produce feedback rather than hard-coded outcomes:

- Order conversion uses a utility $U_{order}$ passed through a sigmoid. Higher delivery fee and longer ETA push conversion down; larger discounts push it up.
- Cancellation is not "re-draw a final cancel probability every minute." It is a per-minute hazard that accumulates with wait and delay, with a hard cutoff at each customer's personal tolerance.
- Rider acceptance depends on bonus, pickup/delivery distance, fatigue, and weather. Dispatch is fixed nearest-rider-first; on rejection, try the next rider.
- Merchant prep time grows with the backlog. The more queued orders, the longer riders wait and the higher the cancel/timeout risk — i.e. peak-hour "burst."
- Platform economics are recognized **only on completed orders**: $\text{Profit}=\text{commission}+\text{delivery fee}-\text{user discount}-\text{rider bonus}-\text{fulfillment cost}$. This sentence looks mundane; later it turns out to matter a lot.

Under the same seed, every policy shares the identical exogenous request stream, so differences between policies come from decisions, not from one policy lucking into a richer request batch.

## 2. Three families of pricing policies

The environment implements seven comparable policies, which fall into three groups:

| Family | Policy | Granularity | Idea |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rule | `no_pricing` / `fixed_pricing` | global constant | baseline / uniform promo |
| Rule | `distance_rule` / `surge_pricing` | per-request | price by distance / by supply-demand ratio |
| Heuristic | `mckp_pricing` | single request | enumerate fee × discount × bonus, score |
| Optimization | `rolling_lagrangian` / `rolling_mip` | zone × 30-min × distance bucket | multi-model prediction + Lagrangian / CP-SAT |

The rule policies are self-explanatory. MCKP enumerates a set of price actions for each latent request, estimates conversion, acceptance, completion, cancellation, timeout, ETA and expected profit for each, computes incremental value over `keep`, and picks the best after fulfillment, budget, ROI and dominance pruning. It responds **per request** — flexible, but each request is scored in isolation with no coordination across zones or windows.

Rolling MIP was the one I had high hopes for: lift the decision unit to zone × window × distance bucket, use a set of prediction models to estimate demand, supply, ETA, cancellation, profit and risk for every candidate strategy bundle, then hand it to OR-Tools CP-SAT to solve jointly under budget, capacity, risk and price-smoothing constraints.

## 3. How the Rolling MIP is formulated

A decision unit is $i=(\text{zone},\text{time\_window},\text{distance\_bucket})$. Each unit enumerates bundles such as `keep`, lower/raise price, user coupon, rider incentive, balanced, rain-day protection, long-distance control and burst control. The binary variable $x_{ik}\in\{0,1\}$ marks whether unit $i$ picks bundle $k$, with objective:

$$\max \sum_i\sum_k x_{ik}\,(\text{ContributionMargin}_{ik}-\beta\,\text{Risk}_{ik})$$

Constraints include: one bundle per unit; three budget caps (user / rider / total); hard filters on ETA, cancellation and burst risk; a zone-window capacity constraint; and price smoothing across adjacent windows and adjacent zones. There is also a safety net by design: if the solve is infeasible or times out, fall back to the Lagrangian approximation, and if that still fails, fall back to `keep`.

Up to this point everything looked reasonable. Then I ran the scale stress test.

## 4. The faceplant: the smarter it got, the more it lost

I scaled the scenario from base (30×30 / 80 merchants / 120 riders / 180 min) up to large (80×80 / 400 merchants / 700 riders / 720 min). Rolling MIP's profit delta against the no-pricing baseline was:

| Scenario | Rolling MIP | Baseline | Delta |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| base | ¥4,918 | ¥4,941 | -¥23 |
| medium | ¥18,137 | ¥19,243 | **-¥1,106** |
| large | ¥59,136 | ¥61,375 | **-¥2,239** |

The larger the scenario, the larger the loss. Inspecting the chosen strategies, the share of `lower_price` climbed from 53.8% in the base scenario to 97.5% in the large one — the MIP was cutting price almost everywhere.

This was not the solver misbehaving. It was optimizing the problem I gave it very diligently. The problem was that I had given it the wrong one.

## 5. Four root causes, none of them the solver

Pulled apart, four specification errors compounded:

1. **Cancelled orders were booked at full revenue.** The original economics formula credited commission and delivery fee regardless of whether the order completed. So "lower the price to pull in more orders" was profitable on paper no matter what — cancelled orders still counted.
2. **Price elasticity was overestimated.** The model's demand response to a price cut had no cap; roughly each ¥1 cut predicted ~30% more demand. Of course the optimizer loved cutting price.
3. **The capacity constraint was effectively absent.** Effective supply was already a window-level capacity, but the constraint multiplied it by 30 again, making it so loose that "pulling in more orders than the system can fulfill" was never penalized.
4. **Cancellation was never calibrated.** In the large scenario the realized cancel rate was ~36.7% while the model predicted ~26.3% — a full 10 points low. Risk was systematically understated.

Put together, the picture is clear: the optimizer saw a world where "cutting price pulls in a flood of demand, the cancelled orders still pay, and the system can absorb the volume." Its optimal solution in that world was to cut price everywhere — and the real environment answered with collapsing fulfillment and lost profit, because that world does not exist.

The fix mapped one-to-one onto these: recognize revenue/subsidy/cost only on expected completed orders; let cancelled orders incur only a cancellation loss; cap the price-elasticity response; drive the rolling state from the most recent window's *realized* conversions rather than latent requests; online-calibrate predicted cancellation and ETA to the most recent window's actuals; rewrite the capacity constraint as "standard-order service-time consumption must not exceed window capacity" and drop the stray ×30. One more data fix: the Uplift data now generates paired coupon/no-coupon potential outcomes per sample.

## 6. Post-fix results

Re-trained and re-compared on the medium scenario (50×50 / 180 merchants / 300 riders / 360 min) over three shared seeds:

| Policy | Avg profit | Completion | Cancellation | Timeout | Avg subsidy |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Rolling MIP | ¥19,295 | 63.57% | 32.80% | 28.35% | ¥0 |
| MCKP | ¥19,286 | 64.36% | 32.08% | 29.01% | ¥626.67 |
| No pricing | ¥19,243 | 63.61% | 32.94% | 27.73% | ¥0 |
| Rolling Lagrangian | ¥19,186 | 63.31% | 33.11% | 27.44% | ¥0 |

Rolling MIP against itself is the cleaner comparison: average profit rose from ¥18,137 to ¥19,295, the delta vs. baseline went from -¥1,106 to +¥52, completion rose from 60.58% to 63.57%, and cancellation fell from 35.73% to 32.80%. The paired per-seed profit uplift is below — dots are individual seeds, the diamond is the mean:

More importantly, the strategy mix changed — that is the real evidence the fix took hold:

![Post-fix Rolling MIP strategy mix](/images/Blog25Fig2.png)

`lower_price` dropped from a once-overwhelming majority to **3.3%**; the workhorses became `keep` (52.7%) and `long_distance_control` (41.8%), with an average delivery fee around ¥3.01. In other words, the optimizer stopped trying to make money from an imaginary price-cut bonanza and started trading distance control and hold-steady decisions for more stable economics.

I want to be careful here, though: **with only three seeds, this gap is nowhere near statistically significant.** Rolling MIP beats MCKP by just ¥9 on average and the baseline by ¥52; it exceeds the baseline on 2 of 3 seeds versus 1 of 3 for MCKP, while MCKP still has a slightly higher completion rate. Rolling MIP's genuine strength is its profit standard deviation (~¥84 vs. ~¥164 for the baseline) — it is more stable. But declaring "joint optimization wins" now would be irresponsible.

## 7. What the failure taught me

A few lessons I expect to keep revisiting:

- **More complex models do not automatically do better.** An extra prediction layer and a solver are just a few more places to get things wrong. Until the inputs were aligned, the "advanced" MIP and Lagrangian produced results that were almost identically bad.
- **The optimizer is an amplifier of input bias.** A 10-point overestimate of elasticity, or a revenue term that forgets to exclude cancelled orders, may look harmless in offline metrics; once inside the objective they get amplified into a catastrophic "cut price everywhere" policy.
- **High offline R² does not mean a model is safe to optimize.** Demand R²=0.999 and conversion R²=0.997 look great, but they only prove the model learned the synthetic DGP. The thing worth watching is the **prediction-vs-realized closed-loop** error, especially for the quantities the optimizer keeps exploiting.
- **Always keep a safe fallback.** The `keep` candidate and the rule baselines saved me here — at worst the system can revert to an interpretable, money-not-losing state.

## 8. What is still unfinished

Honestly, the remaining homework: the post-fix large 720-minute scenario has not been re-run; the medium scenario has only three seeds, and a real conclusion needs at least 20 with paired confidence intervals; there is no explicit user segmentation (new / member / price-sensitive) yet, no multi-window budget lookahead, and no cross-zone rider repositioning; in some windows the MIP still falls back to the Lagrangian, and the infeasibility/timeout causes need their own analysis.

Next I plan to push the medium scenario to 20+ seeds to pin down significance, optimize the zone indexing, and then re-run the large scenario. Only once Rolling MIP stably beats the baseline across many seeds and at large scale would I consider layering reinforcement learning on top for cross-window budget and risk control — rather than piling on yet another more complex model right now.

---

In the end, the most valuable part was not "Rolling MIP finally turned positive," but the failure itself: it forced me to re-audit every link on the chain of *pricing action → customer/rider response → fulfillment → profit*. That may be the biggest use of a simulator like this — make the expensive mistakes first, on synthetic data, rather than losing real money in production.
