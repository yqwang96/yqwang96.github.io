I have recently been building a local-first LLM Wiki. The point is not to make another interface where people upload files and chat with them. The point is to turn material scattered across Notion, local documents, coding sessions, and conversations into a knowledge base that can be read, traced, updated, and used for further reasoning.

The most direct inspiration is Andrej Karpathy’s April 2026 [LLM Wiki idea file](https://gist.github.com/karpathy/442a6bf555914893e9891c11519de94f). Its central observation resonates with me: many RAG systems rediscover knowledge from raw documents for every question. They retrieve chunks, assemble context, and produce a new answer. Even when an answer is useful, its synthesis often disappears with the chat session instead of becoming part of what the system knows next time.

LLM Wiki changes the state of knowledge work rather than the capability of the model itself: **knowledge should move from repeatedly retrieved context to a persistent compiled artifact.**

## From rediscovery to compilation

The conventional RAG path is familiar:

```text
Raw documents → chunking and embeddings → retrieval at query time → LLM answer
```

It is useful for immediate recall across a large and heterogeneous corpus. But its knowledge is assembled just in time. A subtle question that depends on five reports, two meeting notes, and an old decision may require the model to find the evidence and rebuild the synthesis every time.

Karpathy’s alternative places a Markdown wiki, maintained by an LLM, between raw material and the final answer. When a new source arrives, the model does not merely index it. It reads the material, extracts what matters, updates relevant topic pages, adds links, flags contradictions, and writes the revised synthesis into a durable collection of pages.

```text
Raw material → LLM compilation and maintenance → linked topic wiki → queries and further evolution
```

The wiki is therefore not a presentation layer. It is a persistent intermediate knowledge layer. Karpathy offers a useful analogy: Obsidian is the IDE, the LLM is the programmer, and the wiki is the codebase. The human selects worthwhile sources, asks good questions, checks results, and sets priorities. The model performs the recurring work of summarising, filing, cross-referencing, and maintaining consistency.

The word *compilation* matters here. A wiki is neither a copy of the source material nor an archive of chat logs. It is a structured, revisable artifact derived from the material.

## Karpathy’s three layers, and the layer I want to add

Karpathy describes three core layers:

```text
Raw sources  Immutable original material and the source of truth
Wiki         LLM-maintained pages, indexes, links, and synthesis
Schema       The operating conventions that guide an agent's work
```

My project follows this direction, but adds a `sources/` layer between raw material and the wiki:

```text
Collector
    ↓
raw/       Original or near-original content, with URLs, paths, dates, and attachments
    ↓
sources/   Conservatively cleaned canonical sources, with metadata, checksums, and provenance
    ↓
wiki/      Readable pages for concepts, projects, people, decisions, and questions
```

This is not an extra directory for its own sake. It creates a boundary between synthesis and verification.

`raw/` is allowed to be messy because it preserves the evidence. `sources/` cleans, classifies, deduplicates, and standardises only as much as necessary, without pretending to settle the interpretation. `wiki/` is where cross-source synthesis is allowed: topic pages, relationships, and provisional conclusions. In this project, `AGENTS.md` plays the role of Karpathy’s schema: it defines what the Collector, Source Agent, and Wiki Agent may do and what they should avoid.

For a small personal notebook, raw material can probably flow directly into a wiki. But once the corpus contains meetings, project documents, coding sessions, and web material, a canonical-source layer is valuable. It gives a wiki claim a route back to a source section and then to its raw package.

## The problem is not storing more material

Knowledge-management tools are not scarce. The difficult question is whether a growing collection can become useful context rather than a larger pile of things to search.

I want this LLM Wiki to do at least four things.

### 1. Turn sessions into reusable knowledge

Coding-agent sessions often contain valuable information: why an architecture was chosen, how a failure was actually diagnosed, why a model failed, or which commands and validations worked. Yet this knowledge is usually trapped in JSONL files, terminal output, or chat history. The next task starts by searching for it again.

The project includes collectors for Claude Code and Codex sessions. They compress and chunk the conversation while retaining links to the project, time, and original session. The intended result is not only a summary of one session. It is a set of reusable project context, decisions, open questions, and lessons that can be compiled into the wiki.

That is why an LLM Wiki is more valuable than a one-off chat summary: a summary is an ending; a wiki is the starting point of the next piece of work.

### 2. Make answers traceable, not merely plausible

When LLMs write knowledge pages, fluent prose is easy to mistake for established fact. This is why traceability comes first in this project. Raw packages preserve provenance; canonical sources preserve cleaned content and metadata; wiki pages cite sources; and a query can resolve a citation back through the source to the raw package.

This does not make a model infallible. It does make an error easier to locate and correct. Uncertain, contradictory, or under-specified material should not silently become a fact. It belongs in `wiki/questions/`, where the unknown is represented as part of the system’s knowledge.

### 3. Maintain the knowledge base like a codebase

Karpathy emphasises indexes, logs, and linting. The idea feels close to software engineering: it is not enough to create pages. The system must be able to identify stale claims, broken links, orphan pages, duplicate concepts, missing cross-references, and unsupported conclusions.

The project already has the foundations for wiki validation, review queues, and a link graph. Their purpose is not procedural overhead. It is an acknowledgment that a wiki decays. As material accumulates, the object that needs maintenance is not an individual page but the relationships between pages, conclusions, and evidence.

Success should not mean “the LLM generated many Markdown files.” A better criterion is that every ingestion pass makes visible what needs review, which links are broken, and which questions still lack evidence.

### 4. Let good questions and answers compound too

Another important point in Karpathy’s proposal is that a useful query result should not disappear when a chat ends. A comparison, a decision analysis, or a newly discovered connection can become a new page or an update to an existing page.

The knowledge base is therefore not a one-way pipeline in which files enter and answers leave. It is a loop:

```text
Collect material → compile the wiki → ask against the wiki → produce new analysis
      ↑                                                      ↓
      └────────────── write it back after review ───────────┘
```

Writing back must not be unconditional. Decisions, evaluations, forecasts, and cross-source inferences need provenance, an explicit status as synthesis, and a human review path. LLMs are well suited to maintaining structure; they should not be treated as the final arbiter of truth.

## This is not anti-RAG; it adds a time dimension to RAG

I do not think LLM Wiki replaces every RAG system.

Retrieval remains essential for very large or fast-changing corpora, for long-tail information, and for questions that require exact quotations, precise data, or the latest external state. Those cases must return to the source, and sometimes to a live webpage or database. Karpathy himself presents index-first navigation in the context of a moderately sized wiki and suggests adding search tools when they become necessary.

A more useful framing is this: RAG is good at answering *what should I find now?* LLM Wiki is good at preserving *what have we already learned and organised?* The former solves recall; the latter solves accumulation. A practical system will often use both: the wiki holds high-signal, long-lived synthesis, while retrieval supports evidence lookup, incremental enrichment, and scale.

## Where the project is today

The project now has the basic pipeline: collectors, source normalisation, wiki compilation, review, validation, and a citation-aware query entry point. It has explicit boundaries between the layers and can accept material from several channels.

But a working pipeline is not the same as a finished knowledge base. The valuable work still lies ahead: choosing the first sources worth keeping, ingesting a small batch, checking whether each cleaning and synthesis step is trustworthy, repairing indexes and links, and testing whether the wiki makes real questions faster, more accurate, and more reusable than searching through raw chats.

The next priority is therefore not adding more channels or more elaborate agents. It is completing one small, end-to-end loop:

```text
A small batch of real material
    → raw/source/wiki
    → human review of evidence chains and page relationships
    → queries based on real questions
    → reviewed analysis written back into the wiki
```

If that loop works, knowledge will compound with the work. If it does not, more collectors, vector stores, and automation will only create a larger backlog of material to organise.

## The human–LLM division of labour is still the point

The part of Karpathy’s idea I value most is that it does not remove people from knowledge work. It changes the division of labour.

Humans are responsible for:

- choosing material worth admitting to the system;
- deciding which questions matter;
- reviewing consequential synthesis and decisions;
- setting the domain structure, boundaries, and quality bar.

LLMs are responsible for:

- reading, cleaning, filing, and linking material;
- maintaining indexes and citations and surfacing contradictions;
- drafting artifacts that can be revised;
- reducing the maintenance cost enough that the knowledge base stays alive.

The ideal outcome is not a black box that thinks on my behalf. It is an external memory system that keeps work traces organised, preserves evidence, makes uncertainty visible, and helps the next round of thinking begin from somewhere better.

That is why I want this project to be an LLM Wiki, rather than just another chat-with-files tool.
